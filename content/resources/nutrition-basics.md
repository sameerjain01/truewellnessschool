Good nutrition doesn't have to be complicated, expensive, or restrictive. The most powerful nutritional shifts are often the simplest — and the ones most likely to stick.

## The Whole-Food Principle

Whole foods are foods that are as close to their natural state as possible: vegetables, fruits, legumes, whole grains, nuts, seeds, eggs, and high-quality proteins. These foods provide the nutrients, fiber, and phytonutrients your body and brain need to function at their best.

Processed foods — those with long ingredient lists, added sugars, refined flours, and artificial additives — tend to crowd out whole foods and contribute to inflammation, energy crashes, and poor gut health over time.

You don't have to eliminate processed foods entirely. But gradually increasing the proportion of whole foods in your family's meals makes a meaningful difference.

## A Practical Framework: The Plate Method

For each meal, aim for:
- **Half the plate:** vegetables and/or fruit
- **A quarter:** whole grains or starchy vegetables (brown rice, sweet potato, oats, quinoa)
- **A quarter:** quality protein (beans, lentils, eggs, fish, poultry, meat)
- **Plus:** a small amount of healthy fat (olive oil, avocado, nuts)
- **Plus:** water as the primary beverage

This isn't a strict rule — it's a simple visual guide that most families can apply immediately.

## Small Changes That Make a Real Difference

You don't need to overhaul your entire diet at once. Small, consistent shifts compound over time:

- **Add a vegetable before you subtract anything else.** Add spinach to eggs, carrots to pasta sauce, broccoli to stir-fry.
- **Drink more water.** Most people are mildly dehydrated throughout the day, which affects energy, focus, and mood.
- **Reduce sugary beverages.** Sodas, juice drinks, and sweetened coffees are one of the single largest sources of excess sugar in most families' diets.
- **Cook at home more often.** Home-cooked meals, even simple ones, are almost always more nutritious than restaurant or processed options.

## Teaching Children About Food

Children who learn about food from an early age — where it comes from, how to prepare it, what different foods do for the body — develop a healthier relationship with eating that tends to persist into adulthood.

Involve children in shopping, cooking, and age-appropriate food preparation. Let them make choices within healthy boundaries. Avoid using food as reward or punishment.
